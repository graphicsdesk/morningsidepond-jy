At the southern end of Morningside Park, cascading rocks and hanging tree branches shade a murky community pond—the former excavation pit for Columbia’s 1968 attempt to build a largely private gymnasium on public land. Since last summer, the pond has become a research site where University scientists have made progress on University President Minouche Shafik’s initiative to tackle the pond’s harmful algae and broken waterfall.

The pond and waterfall, constructed in 1989 from the foundational crater of the abandoned construction of Columbia’s gym, quickly became a highlight for the community. However, in recent years, many New York City ponds—including the Morningside Park pond—have run the risk of facing harmful algae blooms that cause health risks if ingested or touched, leaving young children and pets particularly susceptible.

The pond’s broken waterfall and water pump system, which were restored in 2018 but quickly broke down a year later, have amplified the algae blooms, leading many West Harlem community members to call on the New York City Department of Parks and Recreation for a permanent restoration.

When Shafik entered her presidency in summer 2023, she saw the pond as an opportunity to engage the local community. At an event in Morningside Park on July 15, Shafik announced a collaboration between New York City Parks and the Climate School to study and solve the algae problem.

While Columbia scientists are pleased with Shafik’s involvement in the park’s restoration, some community leaders have contrastingly noted a lack of communication from the president regarding other West Harlem issues, such as education and affordable housing.

Now, around six months after Shafik’s announcement, Dr. Joaquim Goes, the lead researcher for the algae study, and Dr. Adrian Brügger, the civil engineer leading the waterfall project, have made discoveries on their respective projects that may restore the pond by summer.

“We were energized because there were a lot of Columbia students and also high school students who were in the park that day, who expressed interest in coming to work in my lab to try and contribute to this effort,” Goes said about the announcement event in July.

Over the summer, Goes developed a solution of natural minerals with a team of five New York City high school students that cleared sample pond water of the toxic algae without harming beneficial organisms.

Earlier that year, Minju O’Rourke, SEAS ’25, co-president of the Columbia Space Initiative, had approached Goes with an autonomous boat that her club had created for another project and was not in use. She thought the boat could be used for water quality monitoring.

“We were a couple months in and just got the awesome news that not only were the parks interested, and then later that Minouche and Columbia were investing in being part of this project in Morningside Park,” O’Rourke said. “So we’ve since pivoted all of our work to target Morningside Park.”

Goes and his team hope to equip the boat with environmental sensors and a water sampling system, which then automatically dispense the substrate of natural minerals that Goes’ lab has created. The restoration of the water pumps, which will circulate the pond water along with autonomous boats, will mitigate the algae blooms, Goes said.

Initially, Brügger discovered that part of the pump system, which recirculates the water and supplies water to the waterfall, was never installed, leaving the pumps to burn out.

“So having discovered this, the pumps will be recalibrated so that they don’t burn out, and they should last a long time,” Brad Taylor, president and board chair of Friends of Morningside Park, said. “So that was very heartening.”

However, both still need further approval and permits from New York City Parks to carry out their discoveries.

“We are thankful for our collaboration with the faculty and students at Columbia University as we work together to restore the waterfall and the pond at Morningside Park,” Chris Clark, press officer for New York City Parks, wrote in a statement to Spectator. “We truly appreciate the time and resources they are devoting to this invaluable effort. When this effort to deal with the harmful algal bloom at Morningside is successfully concluded, we will look to see if it’s applicable in other locations.”

Taylor said that this application could have “huge implications” in combating harmful algae blooms in both Morningside Park and the rest of the city.

“I think everyone has a community park that has algae—the lake that I grew up swimming in summer camp in California has been closed for the last two years because of algae problems,” O’Rourke said. “And I think every community, no matter where you’re from, urban and rural, there’s a park near you, or a pond near you with algae. So lots of excitement and pulls towards applying in different communities.”

As Columbia originally sought to build a gymnasium with separate entrances for Harlem residents and University affiliates, O’Rourke sees the project as a way for Columbia to begin to mend their fraught history with the park and Harlem residents.

“I’m glad that the waterfall, which is a loved aspect of the park for a bunch of children and families and community members, is one of the tangential efforts to do some good in ways that Columbia has not previously done,” O’Rouke said.

The gym that Columbia broke ground on in 1968 would have had one entrance for the Harlem community at the bottom of the building and another for University members at the top. Columbia abandoned its controversial plans after five University buildings were occupied for nearly a week by around 1,000 student and community protesters.

For Goes, restoring the park is particularly important given the racial and economic disparities between the East and West side of the park that border it. He sees a unique opportunity for Columbia to aid its community in its work to restore the pond.

“We have this University sitting in this natural divide, and we can be ambassadors to bridge that divide by bringing equity in all the social incentives that the city has to offer,” Goes said.

Taylor, Goes, and O’Rourke cite Shafik’s enthusiasm and involvement as reaffirming her commitment to positive community relations.

“A few days ago, she came to our institute at Lamont,” Goes said. “And you know, one thing about her is that she comes across as someone who was so open, transparent, and there’s no, no hidden agenda.”

Given Shafik’s support for the project, Goes is hopeful about continuing the research effort as a larger Columbia initiative to benefit New York City.

“So it becomes a citywide project. When you have high school students and undergraduates involved, also, can you imagine, this could become a huge movement,” Goes said. “I am hoping we continue, with the president’s support of course, to continue to make this kind of a movement.”
